Mystic Wood is a fantasy adventure for 2 to 4 players, set in an ever-changing
labyrinth somewhere between Earth and Heaven.
Each player chooses as his or her champion a Knight, who sets forth from
the Earthly Gate to fulﬁl a personal quest. Moving by turns along twisting
paths, the Knights explore the Mystic Wood. In some locations, they en-
counter the people and creatures who inhabit the Mystic Wood. Through
encounters with these denizens and jousts with other Knights, the Knights
may acquire strength, prowess, companions, and useful things to help them
fulﬁl their quests. But there are risks as well, including imprisonment in the
Tower.
A player wins the game by fulﬁlling his or her quest and leaving the Wood
bytheEnchantedGate. Alternatively,anyKnightexceptBritomartandMarﬁsa
canwinbydefeatingtheKingandoccupyingtheCastle. AlthoughBritomart
and Marﬁsa cannot win in this way, they have the special ability of being able
to ignore the King.

8.1 Challenge
To resolve a challenge:
1. Roll the two dice. The white die gives your basic score, and the red die
gives the Denizen’s basic score.
2. To the Denizen’s score, add its total strength and prowess.
3. Toyourscore,addeitheryourstrength,yourprowess,orboth,depend-
ing on which powers the Denizen has. Include appropriate bonuses
from other cards you hold such as Ox-slayer, Lance, or Horse. See Ex-
amples below.
4. If the score is tied, re-roll until the challenge is resolved.
5. If your Knight’s score is higher, follow the instructions on the card.
6. If the Denizen’s score is higher, you are immediately imprisoned in the

Tower and your turn ends. Your Companions (see10) are left in the
area with the Denizen and become independent again, but you keep
all other cards (including Horse).
Exception: A Knight who is vanquished by Enchantress (see 18.7) remains in
the area but does not approach any other Denizens. Any Companions become
independent.
Examples: Britomart, carrying the Lance (+1 strength), has 3 prowess and 2
strength. She challenges the Orc, who has a strength of 4. Add 2 to her die roll and
4 to the die roll of the Orc. Britomart’s prowess is ignored because the Orc has only

14 Power Limit
The code of chivalry forbids any Knight to grow too powerful. At the end of
any turn when your Knight’s combined strength and prowess exceed 10, you
must surrender a card or cards of your choice to bring the total to 10 or less.
Prince and Sage, whose aid can be used only once, do not count toward
total strength and prowess in this situation. However, Princess, Grail, and
Horse do.
Surrendered cards are treated as follows:
• Things are left in the area and remain Things. For example, Lance does
not become Wizard.
• Anyothercards,includingCompanions,aretransported. Prowesscards
resume their original form as Denizens. For example, Boar-Slayer be-
comes Wild Boar. Any Knight in the area where these Denizens are
transported must approach them on the next turn, as usual.
Example: Whe turn order is Parsifal, George, Britomart. Parsifal, whose combined
power is 10, enters an area occupied by the other two Knights. He challenges George
(who holds Grail) and defeats him. Parsifal claims as his prize the right to approach
Grail, which he obtains, making his combined powers 12. T o meet the limit, Parsifal
discards Ox-slayer (causing Ox to appear in the corresponding area in the other half of
the W ood) and either drops Ring or gives it to one of the other Knights. If Parsifal drops
Ring, George can pick it up on his turn.

22 The Literary Background
The game was largely inspired by two romantic epics of the 16th century: Ar-
iosto’sOrlando Furioso(Mad Roland) and Spenser’sWhe Faerie Queene. The
ﬁrst is set inan imaginary Europe of Charlemagne, the second inthe realm of
a certain Queen Gloriana, and both poems concern the adventures of various
Knights in search of love and glory.
The poems are immensely long – at that, Spenser’s is unﬁnished and Ar-
iosto’s just a continuation of another long work by Boiardo – and diverse in
matterandmood. Atthetimeoftheirwritingtheymusthavebeenreadaloud
in polite circles, a canto a night, furnishing the kind of entertainment later
generations have found in novels and television.
The heroes and heroines wander through a sketchily deﬁned landscape
punctuated by occasional palaces andcitiesandpeopled with a hostof minor
characters: wizards, crones, clerics, robbers, royalty, rustics, distressed maid-
ens, and ﬁerce “paynims” or Saracens, slightly resembling the Arab invaders
of Europe. The always interesting and often comic encounters of the Knights
withthesecharactersandwithoneanotherformasuccessionofepisodesonly
loosely connected by a central theme or plot.
